Start With the Wash Tag

The wash tag is where most fakes fall apart. Manufacturers changed the layout, wording and material of these tags almost every season, and counterfeiters rarely get the details right for a specific year.

Look at what languages are printed, where the item was made, and how the care symbols are drawn. An Adidas shirt from 1994 has a very different tag to one from 1998. If a seller shows you the front of a shirt but never the tag, ask for it. A genuine seller will have no problem providing that photo.

Check the Crest

On originals from the 80s and 90s, club crests were usually embroidered directly into the fabric, or applied as a woven patch with dense, even stitching. Turn the shirt inside out: the back of a real embroidered crest looks neat and structured.

Fakes often use printed crests, or embroidery that is loose, uneven, or slightly the wrong shade. Colours are a common giveaway. Compare the crest against photos of the shirt actually being worn in that season, not against modern versions of the badge.

Feel the Fabric

Vintage football shirts were made from heavier, coarser polyester than modern ones. A shirt from 1990 does not feel like a shirt from 2020. If something claims to be thirty years old but feels light, smooth and modern, be careful.

Mesh panels, ventilation holes and knitted collars all changed over the years. These construction details are expensive to replicate accurately, so fakes tend to simplify them.

Sponsor Prints Age

Original sponsor prints from that era were applied with heat transfer and they crack, fade and lift over time. That ageing is a good sign, not a flaw. A thirty year old shirt with a perfectly crisp, glossy sponsor print deserves a second look.

Equally, check the font. Sponsors updated their logos over the years, and a shirt carrying a logo version that did not exist in that season is a clear red flag.

Trust the Seller, Not Just the Shirt

The most reliable protection is buying from someone who knows what they are selling. A serious seller photographs the tags, describes flaws honestly, and can tell you exactly why they believe a shirt is genuine.

At Shirt Société every shirt is checked by hand before it is listed. We photograph the tags, mention every flaw, and state clearly when something is a reissue rather than an original.

Washing

Turn the shirt inside out before it goes anywhere near a machine. This protects the print, the badge and the sponsor from rubbing against the drum.

Wash cold, at 30 degrees or below, on a delicate cycle. Use a mild detergent and skip fabric softener entirely: it breaks down the fibres of older polyester over time. Never use bleach, and never wash a vintage shirt with jeans or anything with a zip.

Drying

Never tumble dry. The heat shrinks old polyester and lifts heat pressed lettering off the fabric. Instead, lay the shirt flat on a towel, reshape it, and let it dry away from direct sunlight.

Sunlight fades colour faster than anything else. A shirt dried on a sunny balcony for a few afternoons can lose noticeably more colour than one dried indoors over a year.

Ironing

Avoid it if you can. If you must, iron inside out on the lowest setting and never place the iron directly on a print, number or badge. A single pass with too much heat can ruin a sponsor print permanently.

Storing

For shirts you wear, a padded hanger is best. Wire hangers stretch the shoulders over time and leave permanent marks.

For shirts you are keeping as part of a collection, folding flat in a drawer or an acid free box is safer. Long term hanging pulls the fabric down under its own weight, especially with heavier vintage material. Keep them out of the loft and the basement: heat and damp are the two things that destroy old shirts.

If You Wear Them

There is nothing wrong with wearing a vintage shirt, and most collectors do. Just wash it less often than a modern one, air it out between wears, and accept that some wear is part of its life. These shirts were made to be worn.

Designers Were Given Freedom

In the early nineties, football shirt design was not yet controlled by global marketing departments. Local designers experimented, and the results were extraordinary: geometric explosions, abstract splatter prints, colours that had no business being on a football pitch.

Look at the goalkeeper shirts of that era, or Germany's 1994 home shirt with its bold diamond pattern. Nothing like it would get approved today, and that is exactly why collectors want them.

The Fit Told a Story

Nineties shirts were boxy, long and generous. That was the style, and it is why a vintage L often fits closer to a modern M. Players wore them loose, and the silhouette became part of how we remember those teams.

That fit has come back into fashion, which is one reason vintage shirts are now worn as everyday clothing rather than only kept in collections.

Sponsors Became Icons

Some sponsor logos are now inseparable from the clubs that wore them. Certain combinations of club, kit and sponsor define an entire period of football, and seeing that logo instantly places you in a specific season.

Modern shirts rotate sponsors frequently, which makes them harder to remember. A nineties shirt often carried the same sponsor for years, and that consistency built the association.

They Marked Unrepeatable Moments

The nineties gave us Italia 90, USA 94 and France 98. Those tournaments are burned into the memory of anyone who watched them, and the shirts are the physical link back to those matches.

A Brazil 1998 home shirt is not just polyester. It carries the memory of that team, that tournament and that final. That is what separates a vintage football shirt from ordinary secondhand clothing.

Scarcity Did the Rest

Shirts from that era were produced in far smaller numbers than today's, and most were worn, washed and eventually thrown away. What survives in good condition is genuinely rare, and it gets rarer every year.

That combination of bold design, cultural memory and real scarcity is why nineties shirts remain the most sought after pieces in the collecting world.

Match Worn vs Match Issued

In the world of football shirt collecting, few terms cause more confusion than match worn and match issued. The distinction is significant in terms of authenticity, rarity and value.

A match worn shirt was physically worn by a player during an official match. These shirts carry visible signs of real game time, and that is precisely what makes them special. They were part of the game.

A match issued shirt was prepared and assigned to a player but never actually worn in the match itself. Match issued shirts are still rare and desirable, but typically command a lower price than a true match worn equivalent.

How to Verify a Match Worn Shirt

  • Original number printing applied directly for the player, not a generic retail addition
  • Player-specific sizing often longer, wider or shorter than the standard retail version
  • Authentic wear patterns from real match time, difficult to replicate convincingly
  • Provenance and documentation photographs, certificates of authenticity, or a documented shirt swap history

What Do They Cost?

Most match worn shirts sell for several hundred to several thousand euros, though the range is wide. It depends on the player, the match, the era and the condition of the shirt. At Shirt Societe we focus on pieces with genuine historical significance and price them fairly for serious collectors.

Why Vintage Football Shirts?

Vintage football shirts sit at the intersection of sport, fashion, design history and personal memory. Unlike many collectibles, they are wearable. Despite strong growth over the past decade, the market has not yet reached the price levels seen in vintage trainers or trading cards. There is still genuine value to be found by collectors who know what they are looking at.

Start Smart

  • Collect what genuinely interests you — shirts connected to clubs, nations or eras you care about
  • Study before you spend — time on forums and Instagram before committing serious money
  • Condition is everything — a shirt in excellent condition is almost always more valuable than a rarer shirt in poor condition
  • Learn to authenticate — reproductions are common. Develop an eye for originals before buying at higher price points

Where to Find Shirts

  • Vintage fairs such as Vintage Fussballborse — inspect in person and speak with sellers who know the history
  • Curated shops such as Shirt Societe — research and verification done for you
  • Vinted and eBay — higher risk but occasional excellent finds for patient buyers
  • Instagram — the most active community for buying, selling and learning

Build Around a Theme

Collections built with intention hold and grow in value. Consider all shirts from a specific club across decades, national team shirts from a single tournament year, or a defined era such as 1988 to 1998.
